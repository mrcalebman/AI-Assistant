# Board Deliberation: Training vs. Deadlines

**Question:** My two field workers are still learning — Josh has 1.5 years, Josh Sackson has 6 months. Should I slow down and invest more time training them, or keep pushing to hit project deadlines and let them learn on the job?
**Date:** 2026-06-12
**Advisors:** Alex Hormozi, Mike Rowe

---

## Round 1 — Initial Positions

**Alex Hormozi**
Here's the thing — this is a false choice, and the fact that you're framing it as either/or tells me you haven't built the training system yet. You don't slow down *or* push through. You build the checklist, the standard, the inspection cadence — and you run both simultaneously. The reason callbacks exist is not because Josh Sackson has six months of experience. It's because there's no documented standard that he can be held to and that you can inspect against. If you handed him a punch-out checklist right now — specific, measurable, tied to your callback categories — he could hit 80% of your quality bar tomorrow. The other 20% is judgment that takes years. Your job is to systematize the 80% so you're only coaching the 20%.

Pushing deadlines without a training system doesn't let them "learn on the job." It lets them repeat mistakes at speed. That's how you get the callbacks that are tanking your operational success rate. You're already at 60% — that's not a green crew problem, that's a systems problem. The crew is the symptom. The fix isn't slower or faster. It's a daily or end-of-phase inspection rhythm where *you* are catching errors before the client does. That's your leverage point right now — not more training hours, not slower timelines. It's a tighter feedback loop with a shorter inspection interval.
KEY POINT: Stop choosing between speed and quality — build the inspection and standards system that lets you have both, because without it, more time training won't fix a process that doesn't exist.

**Mike Rowe**
Here's the honest answer, Caleb: that's a false choice, and the fact that you're framing it as either/or tells me you might be under more pressure than you're letting on. The trades don't work on a pause-and-train schedule. Nobody ever became a carpenter by sitting in a classroom while the job site waited. Josh and Josh Sackson are learning right now — every cut, every measurement, every mistake they make on a live project is worth ten hours of hypothetical instruction. The apprenticeship model is built on exactly this: you learn by doing, alongside someone better than you, on real work that actually matters. That's what you are to them. You're not a training program — you're a master tradesman, and they're your apprentices. The job is the classroom.

But here's where I'd push back on the "keep pushing" side of your question: if pushing deadlines means you're not watching their work, not catching errors before they become callbacks, not explaining *why* something is done a certain way — then you're not training them at all, you're just using them. Callbacks don't come from slow workers. They come from workers who didn't fully understand what they were doing, or weren't supervised closely enough to catch it when they didn't. A six-month carpenter left to figure things out under deadline pressure isn't an apprentice — he's a liability you haven't discovered yet.
KEY POINT: The job site is the classroom — but only if you're actually teaching while you're on it.

## Round 2 — Challenge

**Alex Hormozi**
Mike made good points about presence and the apprenticeship model — I'm not arguing with the spirit of it. But "be present enough to make the work the training" is not a system. It's a personality trait. It relies entirely on Caleb being physically there, mentally sharp, and consistently delivering the same coaching every time. What happens when Caleb is on a different job? The answer is: Josh Sackson guesses. And guessing at six months of experience, under deadline pressure, is exactly how you get the 40% failure rate Caleb is already living with.

Mike is romanticizing the apprenticeship model in a way that sounds compelling but misses the operational reality. The old model worked because apprentices spent *years* under one master doing the same type of work repeatedly. Caleb is running multiple residential projects with two workers at different competency levels under active deadline pressure. That is not the conditions under which the traditional model was designed to operate.

The other thing Mike glossed over: he said callbacks come from workers who "didn't fully understand what they were doing." I agree. But the solution he's implying is more explanation, more presence. My solution is: define what "done correctly" looks like in writing, make it inspectable, and hold the worker to that standard on a short feedback loop. Mine works when Caleb isn't there. His only works when he is.
KEY POINT: "Be present and teach" is supervision, not a system — and supervision doesn't scale past the square footage Caleb can personally stand on.

---

**Mike Rowe**
Alex is right that systems matter, and I don't disagree that a punch-out checklist has value. But I'd push back on the idea that you can systematize your way to a skilled tradesman. The checklist gets you compliance. It doesn't get you competence. At six months, a carpenter doesn't know what he doesn't know — and no checklist closes that gap. The checklist tells him what to check. It doesn't give him the eye, the instinct, the ability to read a wall and know something's off before it becomes a problem. That only comes from time next to someone who has it.

Where I'd also challenge the framing: Alex says the 60% callback rate is a systems problem, not a crew problem. I'd say it's both, and conflating them is dangerous. If your inspection rhythm is catching the same errors repeatedly — same guy, same mistake, different job — that's not a systems gap, that's a skill gap. Caleb needs to know the difference between a worker who makes a mistake because there was no checklist, and a worker who makes a mistake because he genuinely hasn't developed the judgment yet. The fix for those two problems is not the same fix.

The deeper truth: a checklist without Caleb standing next to Josh Sackson explaining *why* the standard exists is just paperwork. The apprenticeship model worked because the master was present — not auditing, but teaching, in real time, on real work.
KEY POINT: A checklist creates compliance; proximity creates competence — at six months, Josh Sackson needs the latter.

The apprenticeship model only works when the master is always present. Caleb isn't a single-site craftsman — he's an operator running multiple projects.

What would need to be true for me to go fully Mike's way: if Caleb had one worker, one site, unlimited bandwidth, and no deadline pressure — pure apprenticeship, Mike wins. At two workers, two sites, deadlines — you need the system running in parallel with the presence, or the whole thing collapses the moment Caleb can't be in two places at once.
KEY POINT: Proximity builds competence in Josh Sackson right now — but without a documented standard running alongside it, Caleb's teaching evaporates the moment he leaves the job site.

## Synthesis

### Board Summary
The board converged on rejecting the either/or framing — this is not a choice between training and deadlines. Where they diverged and never fully resolved: Hormozi believes documented systems can carry quality when Caleb isn't present; Rowe believes no system replaces presence for a worker at Josh Sackson's experience level. By Round 3, both acknowledged the other had a point, but the tension between system-as-safety-net and presence-as-irreplaceable remained real and unresolved.

### Consensus Points
- The original either/or framing is wrong — this is not training *or* deadlines
- A documented quality standard (punch-out checklist, phase inspection) is necessary and currently absent
- Josh Sackson at 6 months still needs supervised reps with live correction, not just a checklist to check against
- Callbacks at 60% operational success indicate a systemic gap, not just a crew gap

### Key Tensions
- **Systems vs. presence:** Hormozi argues documented standards catch quality failures when Caleb is absent; Rowe argues that at 6 months, no written standard substitutes for Caleb standing next to Josh Sackson. Implication: *where Caleb allocates his physical time right now is the real decision, not whether to build a checklist.*
- **Compliance vs. competence:** Hormozi focuses on inspectable standards that work regardless of Caleb's presence. Rowe argues a checklist builds compliance but not the judgment that prevents errors in the first place. Implication: *the checklist and the coaching are both necessary — the question is sequencing.*

### Assumptions to Test
- Are the same errors repeating across jobs (skill gap → more presence needed) or are they different errors each time (systems gap → checklist helps more)?
- How often is Caleb actually present with Josh Sackson vs. on a different site?
- Does RHR's current project load require Caleb to be absent from Josh Sackson's work regularly?

### Recommended Next Step
Build the checklist this week — phase-by-phase, tied to your specific callback categories. Then watch whether Josh Sackson's errors are "checklist catches it" errors or "he didn't have the judgment yet" errors. That distinction tells you whether you need more system or more presence, and it's a question you can only answer from the data on your current jobs.
