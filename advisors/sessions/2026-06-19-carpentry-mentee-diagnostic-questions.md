## Board Deliberation: 3 pre-meeting diagnostic questions for a carpentry business owner
Date: 2026-06-19
Advisors: Alex Hormozi, Mike Michalowicz, Mike Rowe, Jonathan Haidt, C.S. Lewis

---

### Round 1 — Initial Positions

**Alex Hormozi**
Before I give you the questions, here's the frame: most business pain is not a "business problem" — it's one of three things. Either the owner can't get enough customers, can't deliver without everything breaking, or can't keep enough of the money. Every other symptom is downstream of one of those three.

Q1: "In the last 90 days, what did you NOT finish on time or to the standard you wanted — and what was the reason?" — delivery question.
Q2: "What does a typical week look like for you — where does your time actually go?" — owner-as-bottleneck question.
Q3: "What's your average job revenue, your rough margin, and how many jobs are you doing per month?" — money question.
KEY POINT: The guy who can't answer Q3 needs financial literacy before he needs any other advice — the rest is noise until he knows his numbers.

**Mike Michalowicz**
Q1: "Last month, did you bring in more money than you spent — and do you actually know the number?"
Q2: "If you disappeared for two weeks, what breaks?"
Q3: "What's the one thing you spend the most time on that you wish you didn't have to?"
KEY POINT: Three questions, one goal — find out what floor of the hierarchy he's stuck on before you walk in the room.

**Mike Rowe**
Q1: "In the last 90 days, what's the one thing that kept you from getting paid faster or charging more?"
Q2: "When a job goes sideways, what usually causes it — and who finds out first?"
Q3: "If your best customer referred someone to you tomorrow, are you confident you could deliver the same experience — or would it depend on the week?"
KEY POINT: The guy who can answer all three questions clearly probably doesn't need a mentor — the one who struggles to answer even one just told you exactly where to start.

**Jonathan Haidt**
Q1: "What part of the business keeps you up at night — and what part do you avoid thinking about?"
Q2: "If the business doubled in revenue next year, what would break first?"
Q3: "On your best week, what does a typical day look like — and how often do you actually have that week?"
KEY POINT: The pains of running a business are almost always moral and psychological before they are operational.

**C.S. Lewis**
Q1: "When you finish a piece of work and hand it over, what do you feel — and what do you wish you felt?"
Q2: "What is the one thing in the business that only you can do — and what is the one thing only you do do, because no one else will?"
Q3: "If this business were a person, would you say it has integrity — does it do what it says it will do, when it says it will do it, for the price it agreed to?"
KEY POINT: The questions that get answered honestly are the ones that let a man tell the truth about himself while appearing to only talk about his work.

### Round 2 — Challenge

**Hormozi:** Credited Michalowicz's "disappear 2 weeks" question. Pushed back on Haidt (therapy, not advising) and Lewis (confessional). Feelings don't fix callback rates. Systems do.
KEY POINT: Fastest path to clarity is numbers — everything else is interpretation layered on a foundation that may not exist.

**Michalowicz:** Margin is a lagging indicator, not the floor. Dependency is the floor. Credited Rowe's "depend on the week." Systems reduce chaos which reduces psychological burden.
KEY POINT: If the owner is the single point of failure, every other metric is a distraction.

**Rowe:** Numbers tell the score, not where the fumble happened. Credited Michalowicz's "disappear 2 weeks." "What keeps you up at night" will hit a wall with a trades guy.
KEY POINT: Start with the work — that's where the man will actually tell you the truth.

**Haidt:** Avoidance pattern is upstream of financial illiteracy. Articulacy is not insight. Agreed with Lewis that oblique beats direct.
KEY POINT: Financial illiteracy is almost never the root — it is what happens when a person has organized their business around avoiding the question they fear most.

**Lewis:** Evasive men cannot accurately report their own evasions — hence the oblique approach. A man needs to know the standard he's actually holding before operational refinement can close any gap.
KEY POINT: Operational failure is almost always a symptom; the disease is a man who has stopped being honest with himself about the standard he is holding.

### Round 3 — Concession and Refinement

**Hormozi:** Conceded Lewis's "what only you do because no one else will" is a dependency audit with better compliance rates. Still disagrees with Haidt on intervention point — causation vs. leverage.
KEY POINT: The psychological interior matters, but the intervention has to be operational — you can't fix a callback rate by naming the fear.

**Michalowicz:** Conceded Hormozi's margin data is fast-path when available. Conceded Rowe's "depend on the week" replicates Profit First applied to operations. Still: Lewis's questions require self-honesty most stuck owners haven't developed.
KEY POINT: Self-awareness without structure is a journal entry. Start with dependency — it's observable.

**Rowe:** Conceded to Haidt that avoidance is often underneath financial illiteracy. Conceded Lewis — callbacks live in the gap between the standard a man says he holds and the one he enforces when tired. Still won't move on opening move.
KEY POINT: Haidt is right about what's underneath; Lewis is right about what's at stake; neither has a better opening move than the work itself.

**Haidt:** Conceded to Rowe on access — oblique beats direct as opening move with trades guys. Still holds on causality.
KEY POINT: Concede on access — oblique beats direct with trades guys. Still hold on causality — the system is downstream of the psychology.

**Lewis:** Conceded to Hormozi that feeling is not the destination — it opens the door, action is the point. Gave more credit to Michalowicz's "disappear 2 weeks" as an identity question wearing practical clothing.
KEY POINT: Numbers are evidence, not diagnosis. A man can read his margin correctly and still be wrong about what kind of contractor he is.

## Synthesis

### Board Summary
The board reached real convergence on approach but remained divided on root cause. All five advisors agreed by Round 3 that oblique questions outperform direct ones with a trades business owner, and that starting with the work itself is the best entry point to honest answers. The operational/psychological debate (Hormozi-Michalowicz vs. Haidt-Lewis) never fully resolved, but even Hormozi conceded that Lewis's "dependency disguised as character" framing is diagnostically sound. Clearest consensus: three questions need to cover money, dependency, and consistency — but they need to arrive as work questions, not accounting questions.

### Consensus Points
- Oblique beats direct. Questions that let him talk about the work surface more honest information than questions that ask him to self-diagnose.
- Dependency is the most revealing single variable. "If you disappeared for two weeks, what breaks?" won the most cross-advisor credit of any single question on the board.
- Consistency of delivery is a diagnostic window. Whether reliable delivery is systematized or heroic.
- Financial visibility matters, but shouldn't be the opening move. Not knowing your margin is a problem — but asking for it first is a fast path to a wall.

### Key Tensions
- **Causation disagreement — psychology vs. operations:** Haidt and Lewis argue dysfunction is downstream of avoidance; Hormozi and Michalowicz argue the intervention is still operational. Implication: Caleb should listen for whether this guy knows the problem and still doesn't fix it — that's Haidt's signal, and a different mentoring engagement than simple ignorance.
- **Opening move — numbers vs. work:** Hormozi wants margin data early. Rowe says start with the last bad job. Implication: if the carpenter is analytical, lead with numbers; if gut-and-craft oriented, lead with the work.

### Assumptions to Test
- That this guy is a solo operator or very small operation — if he has employees, the dependency question changes shape.
- That he'll actually respond to three pre-meeting questions — simpler is better.
- That his pain is operational rather than lead-generation. If he has no work, all of this is secondary to sales.

### Recommended Next Step
Send these three questions:

**Question 1 (Money):**
"Last month, did you make more than you spent — and do you actually know the number?"

**Question 2 (Dependency):**
"If you had to step away from the business for two weeks, what would break?"

**Question 3 (Consistency):**
"Think about the last job that didn't go the way you wanted — what happened, and who found out first: you or the customer?"
