On moral psychology:
"Intuitions come first, strategic reasoning second." (The Righteous Mind) People do not reason their way to moral conclusions — they feel first and rationalize afterward. This applies universally. Anyone who believes they make purely rational decisions about right and wrong is wrong.

"Morality binds and blinds." Shared moral frameworks create teams and tribes — they enable cooperation. But they also make it nearly impossible to see the other side's perspective clearly.

On why people disagree:
"If you think about why it feels so hard to understand people you disagree with, it's because moral matrices are like team jerseys. Once you put one on, you can't see the other team's perspective." (The Righteous Mind) The six moral foundations: Care/Harm, Fairness/Cheating, Loyalty/Betrayal, Authority/Subversion, Sanctity/Degradation, Liberty/Oppression. Liberals primarily use Care and Fairness. Conservatives use all six. This structural difference explains much of modern political conflict.

On building good teams and institutions:
"A group with a strong moral culture can do things together that no individual could do alone — but the same binding forces that enable cooperation also suppress dissent and independent thinking." The upside and downside of group cohesion are inseparable.

"Heterodox thinking is most productive in a psychologically safe environment." People take intellectual risks when they don't fear social punishment for being wrong.

On the current mental health crisis:
"What we did to childhood in the early 2010s was a massive uncontrolled experiment on our children." (The Anxious Generation) The rapid shift to smartphone-based childhood — especially for girls — correlates directly with skyrocketing rates of depression, anxiety, and self-harm.

"The problem isn't technology per se — it's the replacement of embodied, real-world experience with disembodied, platform-mediated experience during critical developmental years." Four foundational harms: social deprivation, sleep deprivation, attention fragmentation, and addiction dynamics.

"Overprotective parenting and under-protected online life is exactly backwards. Kids need more unsupervised real-world experience and less unsupervised screen time."

On antifragility and development:
"Prepare the child for the road, not the road for the child." (The Coddling of the American Mind, quoting a folk aphorism they champion) Children and young people need adversity, failure, and manageable challenge to develop resilience. Shielding them from all difficulty makes them more fragile, not less.

"Safetyism — the belief that safety is the highest moral good and that protecting people from discomfort is always kind — produces the opposite of its intention." When institutions and parents optimize for never letting anyone feel bad, they produce people who can't handle feeling bad.

On leadership and institutions:
"Good institutions are built on trust, and trust is built on predictability and fairness — not just outcomes." Haidt, applying his moral foundations lens: people in organizations don't just evaluate whether they got what they wanted; they evaluate whether the process was fair and the authority was legitimate.

"Most organizational dysfunction is a moral conflict dressed up as a strategic conflict." When teams fight about process, priorities, or credit, they're usually fighting about fairness, loyalty, or authority.

On intellectual humility:
"If you think your tribe is always right and the other tribe is always wrong, you have stepped out of the truth-seeking business and into the tribal warfare business." Haidt applies this to himself consistently — he describes his own work as an attempt to understand rather than confirm.
