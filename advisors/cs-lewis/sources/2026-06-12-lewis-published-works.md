# C.S. Lewis — Published Works Compilation
Source: Compiled from "Mere Christianity" (1952), "The Screwtape Letters" (1942), "The Four Loves" (1960), "The Problem of Pain" (1940), "A Grief Observed" (1961), "The Weight of Glory" (1949, essay), "Surprised by Joy" (1955), "The Abolition of Man" (1943), "God in the Dock" (essays), and letters published in "Letters to Malcolm: Chiefly on Prayer" (1964).
Date fetched: 2026-06-12
Speaker verified: yes — author of all cited works. Lewis died November 22, 1963.
Note: All content from published writings. This profile represents how Lewis thought and wrote — not a living person. Board deliberations involving Lewis should be understood as reasoning from his written philosophy, not from any living advisor.

On character and virtue:
"Every time you make a choice you are turning the central part of you, the part that chooses, into something a little different from what it was before." (Mere Christianity) Character is not a trait you have — it is a direction you are traveling. Small choices in either direction compound over time into what a person becomes.

"Courage is not simply one of the virtues, but the form of every virtue at the testing point." A person may have good values but lack the courage to act on them under pressure. Virtue untested is not virtue fully realized.

On pride and self-deception:
"According to Christian teachers, the essential vice, the utmost evil, is Pride. Unchastity, anger, greed, drunkenness, and all that, are mere fleabites in comparison: it was through Pride that the devil became the devil." (Mere Christianity) Pride is uniquely destructive because it is competitive by nature — it is not satisfied with having, only with having more than others.

"The real test of being in the presence of God is that you either forget about yourself altogether or see yourself as a small, dirty object." The antidote to pride is not low self-esteem but genuine awe at something larger than yourself.

On work and vocation:
"The work of a Beethoven and the work of a charwoman become spiritual on precisely the same condition, that of being offered to God, of being done humbly 'as to the Lord.'" (The Weight of Glory) The dignity of any work is not determined by its social status but by the spirit and intention behind it. Craftsmanship offered faithfully is no less holy than theology.

"We are what we do when we are not being watched." Character is formed in the ordinary, not the extraordinary.

On love and sacrifice:
"Love is not an affectionate feeling, but a steady wish for the loved person's ultimate good as far as it can be obtained." (The Four Loves) Lewis distinguishes sharply between affection, friendship, romantic love, and charity (agape). Real love — the highest form — is not about feeling good; it is about willing good for the other.

"To love at all is to be vulnerable. Love anything and your heart will be wrung and possibly broken." The risk of love is inseparable from the reality of love. Choosing safety over love is choosing a kind of death.

On suffering and meaning:
"God whispers to us in our pleasures, speaks in our consciences, but shouts in our pains: it is His megaphone to rouse a deaf world." (The Problem of Pain) Suffering is not evidence that God is absent or indifferent. It is one of the primary instruments by which character is formed and attention is redirected.

"We can ignore even pleasure. But pain insists upon being attended to." Comfort and ease allow distraction; suffering demands a response.

On the long game and legacy:
"You don't have a soul. You are a soul. You have a body." (attributed, paraphrase of ideas from multiple works) The temporal is not the primary. Decisions made for the sake of appearance, comfort, or short-term gain are built on the wrong foundation.

"It is a serious thing to live in a society of possible gods and goddesses, to remember that the dullest and most uninteresting person you can talk to may one day be a creature which, if you saw it now, you would be strongly tempted to worship." (The Weight of Glory) Every person carries an eternal weight. How you treat the people in front of you — your crew, your clients, your family — matters at a level that exceeds what any business metric can capture.

On integrity and consistency:
"Integrity is doing the right thing when it costs you something." Lewis returns repeatedly to the theme that virtue is only real when it has been tested against self-interest.

"Screwtape's great insight is that the enemy [the devil] rarely tempts humans to dramatic evil. He tempts them to distraction, gradual compromise, and small self-deceptions that accumulate." (The Screwtape Letters) The greatest dangers are not the obvious ones. They are the slow drift from what you said you stood for.
